My error dashboard showed 100% success. Meanwhile, a chunk of my Claude Fable 5 requests were coming back with zero content. No 4xx, no 5xx, no exception — just a successful HTTP 200 with an empty response body and a `stop_reason` I had never handled before.

That is the single most important thing to understand about Claude Fable 5 before you migrate anything: **refusals are not errors**. They are successful responses, and if your monitoring only watches status codes, you are flying blind.

I have been running Fable 5 in production workloads since it came back online on July 1 — after a three-week suspension that deserves its own section — and this is the review I wish I had read on day one. Not a benchmark recap. What actually breaks, what the refusal fallback code looks like, and when the 2x price over Opus 4.8 is worth it.

## What Claude Fable 5 Actually Is

Anthropic shipped Claude Fable 5 (`claude-fable-5`) on June 9, 2026 as the first model in the Claude 5 family — and the first of a new **Mythos-class tier** that sits above Opus in capability. It has a 1M-token context window (the maximum is also the default), 128K max output tokens, and pricing at **$10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output** — exactly double Opus 4.8's $5/$25.

The naming needs a moment because it confused everyone, including me. Fable 5 and **Claude Mythos 5** are the *same underlying model*. The difference is safeguards: Fable 5 ships with safety classifiers that redirect certain categories of requests; Mythos 5 runs without them and is only available to Project Glasswing partners — a government-adjacent program giving cyberdefenders access for vulnerability discovery. If you are not in Glasswing, Fable 5 is the model you get.

Those classifiers target three areas: offensive cybersecurity, biology and chemistry, and attempts to extract the model's raw reasoning. Anthropic says they trigger in under 5% of sessions on average. That number matters later.

## The Benchmarks — and Which Ones to Trust

The headline numbers are genuinely impressive:

- **SWE-bench Verified: ~95%**, versus 88.6% for Opus 4.8 and 82.6% for GPT-5.5 — this one comes from vals.ai's independent leaderboard, so I trust it
- **SWE-Bench Pro: 80.3%**, about 11 points ahead of the field — but this was measured on Anthropic's own scaffolding, and independent evaluators have contested it, so treat it as directional
- Highest score on Cognition's **FrontierCode** eval, which is independent

The more interesting signal is anecdotal but concrete: Stripe reportedly used Fable 5 to compress months of engineering into days on a 50M-line Ruby migration. That aligns with my experience — the gains are largest on **long-horizon agentic work**, not on tasks Opus already handled well. On a routine "write this endpoint" task, I could not tell the two models apart. On a multi-hour refactor spanning dozens of files with self-verification loops, Fable 5 was noticeably more coherent deep into the run.

## Migration From Opus 4.8: Three Things That Break

The good news: same Messages API, same tool-use patterns, same tokenizer as Opus 4.8, so your token counts and cache budgets carry over roughly unchanged. The migration is mostly a model-ID swap. Mostly.

### 1. Thinking is always on — remove your thinking config

Fable 5 thinks on every request, and you cannot turn it off. An explicit `thinking: {"type": "disabled"}` returns a 400 — which surprised me, because that exact config is *accepted* on Opus 4.8. `budget_tokens` is also gone — the same hard break I documented in my [Opus 4.7 hands-on review](/blog/claude-opus-4-7-developer-review-whats-changed), now taken one step further.

```python
# Opus 4.8 — this worked fine
response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-opus-4-8",
    max_tokens=16000,
    thinking={"type": "disabled"},  # 400 on claude-fable-5
    messages=[...],
)

# Fable 5 — omit thinking entirely, steer depth with effort
response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-fable-5",
    max_tokens=16000,
    output_config={"effort": "high"},  # low | medium | high | xhigh | max
    messages=[...],
)
```

One related change: the raw chain of thought is **never returned**. Thinking blocks arrive with empty text by default. If you surface reasoning to users, set `thinking: {"type": "adaptive", "display": "summarized"}` to get a readable summary — otherwise your streaming UI shows a long, silent pause before output starts.

### 2. Assistant prefill returns a 400

If you are still prefilling the assistant turn to force JSON output, that pattern died in the 4.6 family and stays dead here. Use structured outputs (`output_config.format`) instead.

### 3. Zero data retention orgs are locked out

Fable 5 requires **30-day data retention**. If your organization runs under a ZDR arrangement, every single request returns `400 invalid_request_error` — with a perfectly valid payload. I watched someone burn an hour debugging their request body when the problem was an org-level setting. If your Fable 5 requests 400 instantly and consistently, check your retention configuration before touching your code.

## Handling Refusals: The Code Nobody Shows You

Back to my lying dashboard. Fable 5's safety classifiers can decline a request **before any output** (empty `content` array, nothing billed) or **mid-stream** after partial output (the streamed part is billed — discard it). Either way you get HTTP 200 with `stop_reason: "refusal"` and a `stop_details` object carrying the category.

The minimum viable handling — check `stop_reason` before you ever touch `content[0]`:

```python
response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-fable-5",
    max_tokens=16000,
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
)

if response.stop_reason == "refusal":
    category = response.stop_details.category if response.stop_details else None
    log_refusal(category)  # make refusals VISIBLE in your metrics
else:
    print(response.content[0].text)
```

But the better answer is the **server-side fallbacks** parameter. It is opt-in, and in my opinion it should be in every Fable 5 call you write: on a policy decline, the API transparently re-runs the same request on Opus 4.8 inside the same call. A decline before any output is not billed; the rescue bills at the fallback model's own (cheaper) rates.

```python
response = client.beta.messages.create(
    model="claude-fable-5",
    max_tokens=16000,
    betas=["server-side-fallback-2026-06-01"],
    fallbacks=[{"model": "claude-opus-4-8"}],
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
)

# Which model actually served this? Check usage.iterations
fallback_ran = any(
    entry.type == "fallback_message"
    for entry in (response.usage.iterations or [])
)
if fallback_ran and response.stop_reason != "refusal":
    print(f"Served by fallback: {response.model}")
```

Two operational notes from running this for a week:

- **Track the fallback rate as a first-class metric.** If you drive Claude from your own service — the patterns from my [Agent SDK production guide](/blog/claude-agent-sdk-typescript-production-guide) apply here — treat refusal rate exactly like you treat error rate. Anthropic's "<5% of sessions" is an average. If you work on anything security-adjacent, your rate will be higher — developers have reported that C/C++/Rust work, Win32 API references, and files containing words like "security", "unsafe", or "hook" can trip the classifier. My infrastructure-tooling workload sat around 8% fallback after the July redeployment.
- **A fallback is a silent capability downgrade.** The request succeeds, but Opus 4.8 answered it. For most tasks that is fine. For the long-horizon work you chose Fable 5 for, you want to know it happened — hence the `usage.iterations` check.

## The Three-Week Suspension

This part sounds made up, so here is the timeline: Fable 5 launched June 9. On June 12, Amazon researchers demonstrated a jailbreak that got the model to identify software vulnerabilities and demonstrate exploitation. The US government imposed **export controls** on Fable 5 and Mythos 5 the same day, requiring Anthropic to restrict access by nationality — which they had no way to verify in real time. So they suspended the model. Globally. For everyone.

Access came back July 1 with a retrained classifier that blocks the reported technique in over 99% of cases — and, by Anthropic's own admission, produces more false positives on routine coding tasks. That is the source of the post-redeployment grumbling you have seen on social media, and it is real: the model is measurably more trigger-happy on systems-level code than it was in the first three days.

The architectural lesson cost me nothing to learn because I got lucky, but it would have been expensive otherwise: **never hard-pin a production pipeline to a single model with no fallback path**. For nineteen days, `claude-fable-5` simply did not exist as an endpoint. Teams that had already moved critical workloads onto it and removed their Opus paths were rewriting config under pressure. My rule since: every model ID in production config ships with a tested fallback model next to it, and the switch is a config change, not a deploy.

## The Cost Math

Fable 5 is 2x Opus 4.8 on both input and output. When does that pay off?

The naive comparison — same prompt, same output, twice the price — makes Fable 5 look bad. The real comparison is **cost per completed task**. On my hardest agentic workload (a repo-wide migration with verification loops), Fable 5 finished in fewer turns with fewer dead ends. Fewer turns means fewer input tokens re-sent, fewer tool results re-read, less cache churn. The per-token premium was roughly offset by a ~40% reduction in total tokens for the task — and it finished runs Opus occasionally failed outright, which is the expensive case nobody prices in.

On routine work, the math flips hard. A classification call, a summarization endpoint, a code-review comment: Opus 4.8 does these indistinguishably well at half the price, without the refusal risk, without the ZDR restriction. The tiering logic is the same one I used to [cut my Claude Code bill by 70%](/blog/claude-code-token-optimization-bill-cut-guide) — expensive model for the hard reasoning, cheaper tier for everything else — just with a new rung at the top. One more subtlety: because thinking is always on and billed, even a trivial Fable 5 request carries thinking overhead you cannot disable.

## When to Use Fable 5 (and When Not To)

Use Fable 5 for:

- **Long-horizon autonomous work** — multi-hour agentic runs, large migrations, overnight coding sessions. This is where the Mythos-class gap is real. If you already orchestrate [parallel subagents with Opus 4.8's dynamic workflows](/blog/claude-code-dynamic-workflows-parallel-subagents-guide), Fable 5 as the orchestrator is the single most impactful upgrade
- **Hardest-tier reasoning** — problems where Opus 4.8 produces plausible-but-wrong answers
- **Frontier vision tasks** — it is state-of-the-art on dense and degraded images

Stay on Opus 4.8 for:

- **Routine and high-volume workloads** — half the price, no refusal surface, no retention requirement
- **Security tooling and systems-level code** — legitimate work in these areas has the highest false-positive refusal rate; you would be paying 2x to get silently served by Opus anyway
- **ZDR organizations** — you have no choice; Fable 5 is a hard 400
- **Latency-sensitive paths** — Fable 5 turns run long by design; a 15-minute single request is normal at high effort on hard tasks

## Migration Checklist

Before you flip the model string:

1. Remove all `thinking` configuration (`disabled` and `budget_tokens` both 400)
2. Remove assistant prefills; use `output_config.format` for structured output
3. Confirm your org meets the 30-day retention requirement
4. Add `stop_reason == "refusal"` handling before any `content[0]` access
5. Opt into server-side fallbacks (`server-side-fallback-2026-06-01` + `fallbacks: [{"model": "claude-opus-4-8"}]`)
6. Add refusal/fallback rate to your dashboards — HTTP status codes will not show you anything
7. Plan for minutes-long turns: streaming, timeouts, async progress UX
8. Keep your Opus 4.8 path alive in config — June 12 can happen again

Fable 5 is the most capable model I have ever used, and I mean that literally — and it is also the first model where the operational story matters as much as the capability story. Treat it as your [escalation tier, not your default](/blog/fable-5-claude-code-model-routing-playbook), wire up the fallbacks, and it earns its price on exactly the work that justifies it.

If you migrate one thing this week, make it your refusal handling. Your dashboard is lying to you right now.
