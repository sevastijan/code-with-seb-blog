Then a friend showed me [Fabric](https://fabric.so/?via=codewithseb). "Just throw stuff in," he said. "It figures out the rest." I was skeptical. I've heard the "AI organizes everything" pitch before — usually from tools that sort things into wrong categories with great confidence.

So I tested it properly. Two weeks, real content, real workflows. Here's what I found.

## The Philosophy: "Death to Organizing"

Fabric's core bet is that manual organization is a tax on thinking. Every minute you spend filing a note into the right folder, choosing the right tags, or linking to related content is a minute you're not spending on the actual work.

Their approach: **you capture, AI organizes.** Drop a PDF, save a bookmark, write a note, record a voice memo — Fabric auto-categorizes it, extracts key concepts, and makes it searchable by meaning, not just keywords.

This is a fundamentally different philosophy from Obsidian (manual linking, you build the structure) and Notion (databases and templates, you design the system). Fabric says: don't build a system. Just accumulate. The structure emerges.

## How Semantic Search Changes Everything

The feature that sold me on giving Fabric a serious test: **semantic search**. You search by meaning, not by exact words.

In Obsidian, if I saved a note about "React component rendering performance" and search for "why is my UI slow," I get nothing. The keywords don't match.

In Fabric, that search works. The AI understands that "UI slow" is semantically related to "rendering performance" and surfaces the right note. It feels less like searching a database and more like asking someone who's read everything you've ever saved.

This works across all content types — not just text notes, but inside PDFs, bookmarked articles, even transcribed voice memos. I bookmarked a 40-page whitepaper on caching strategies two months ago. Searching "how to invalidate stale data" found the relevant section on page 23. That's genuinely impressive.

Every Fabric workspace comes with an AI chat assistant. You can ask it:

- "Summarize the key points from that PDF about microservices"

It searches your workspace, finds relevant content, and synthesizes an answer with citations to specific notes and documents. Think of it as ChatGPT, but grounded in *your* knowledge base instead of the internet.

What sets Fabric apart from "just another note app" for developers:

Fabric exposes an [MCP server](/blog/mcp-vs-a2a-ai-agent-protocols-guide-2026) that connects to Claude Code, Cursor, and other AI tools. Your AI agent can search your Fabric workspace and create items directly. I covered MCP integrations extensively in my [developer MCP stack guide](/blog/developer-mcp-stack-multi-tool-workflow-guide) — Fabric fits right into that ecosystem.

### REST API and CLI

Full API access with Python and JavaScript clients. Everything you can do in the UI, you can do programmatically. This matters if you're building [custom agents](/blog/claude-agent-sdk-typescript-production-guide) that need persistent memory — Fabric's API handles storage, indexing, and retrieval.

### Developer Platform

Fabric offers a developer platform specifically for building AI agents with persistent memory. The Memory API, Workspaces API, and Resources API let your agents store and retrieve knowledge across sessions. This is a level above what most note-taking apps offer.

## Honest Pros

**Zero-effort organization actually works.** I dumped 200 items into Fabric over two weeks — notes, bookmarks, PDFs, screenshots. Finding any of them later took seconds. I never created a folder. I never added a tag. The semantic search just works.

**Multi-format support is unmatched.** No other tool I've used handles PDFs, voice memos, videos, bookmarks, and notes equally well. Obsidian is markdown-only. Notion handles databases well but PDFs are second-class citizens.

**Cross-device sync is seamless.** Web, iOS, Android, desktop. Save a bookmark on your phone, find it on your laptop. This sounds basic, but Obsidian's sync story is still painful without paying for Obsidian Sync or setting up Syncthing.

**AI assistant is grounded in your content.** Unlike generic AI chatbots, Fabric's assistant only references things you've actually saved. No hallucinations about content that doesn't exist in your workspace.

## Honest Cons

**No local-first option.** Your data lives on Fabric's servers. It's encrypted (AES-256 at rest, SSL in transit), but if you're in a regulated industry or simply prefer local files, this is a dealbreaker. Obsidian wins here decisively.

**No plugin ecosystem.** Obsidian has 1,400+ plugins. Fabric has... Fabric. What you see is what you get. No Dataview queries, no custom templates, no community extensions.

**Limited manual control.** If you *want* to organize things into a specific structure, Fabric fights you. The whole point is AI organization, but sometimes I know exactly where something should go and I want to put it there. Fabric's "we'll handle it" approach feels limiting in those moments.

**No offline access.** Cloud-dependent. If you're on a plane or somewhere without internet, your knowledge base is inaccessible. Obsidian works fully offline.

**Pricing adds up for power users.** $16/month for Pro is reasonable, but it's $192/year for a note-taking tool. Obsidian is free with $96/year for optional sync. Notion is $10/month. The value needs to justify the premium.

## Who Should Try Fabric

**Good fit:**
- You save lots of different content types (PDFs, bookmarks, screenshots, not just text notes)
- You hate organizing but need to find things later
- You want AI search that actually understands what you're looking for
- You work across multiple devices and need seamless sync
- You're building AI agents that need persistent memory (Developer Platform)

**Not a good fit:**
- You need local-first data storage (regulatory, privacy, or preference)
- You love building custom PKM systems (Obsidian plugins, Notion databases)
- You need deep manual control over your knowledge structure
- You work primarily offline
- You're happy with your current setup and the "organizing tax" doesn't bother you

## Verdict

Fabric doesn't replace Obsidian or Notion — it solves a different problem. Obsidian is for [deep thinking and manual knowledge architecture](/blog/obsidian-developer-second-brain-2026-setup). Notion is for team collaboration and structured databases. Fabric is for people who want to **capture everything and find anything** without spending time on organization.

The semantic search is genuinely best-in-class. The multi-format support is unmatched. The AI assistant is useful, not gimmicky. And the developer platform opens up interesting possibilities for [AI agents with persistent memory](/blog/claude-agent-sdk-typescript-production-guide).

Is it worth the price? If you save more than 50 items a month across different formats and regularly struggle to find them later — yes. If you primarily write text notes and enjoy the organizing process — stick with Obsidian.
